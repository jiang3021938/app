# Tennessee Lease Agreement Guide 2026: What Landlords & Tenants Must Know

![Tennessee Lease Agreement Guide 2026](/assets/tennessee-lease-agreement-hero.webp)

Creating and managing compliant lease agreements in Tennessee requires a solid understanding of the state's landlord-tenant regulations. Tennessee's **Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (URLTA)**, codified in **Tennessee Code §66-28-101 through §66-28-521**, provides a comprehensive framework for rental relationships in the Volunteer State. While Tennessee is generally considered more landlord-friendly than many states, the law still imposes important obligations on property owners and provides significant protections for tenants.

Whether you're drafting your first **Tennessee lease agreement** or reviewing an existing rental contract, understanding the state's requirements is essential to creating enforceable leases that protect both parties' interests. From security deposit handling and maintenance obligations to entry rights and eviction procedures, Tennessee law establishes clear rules that every landlord and tenant should understand.

## Understanding Tennessee's Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act

The **Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (URLTA)** is the foundation of Tennessee's rental housing law. Enacted to provide a standardized framework for landlord-tenant relationships, the URLTA establishes rights and responsibilities for both parties.

### Applicability of the URLTA

The URLTA applies to most residential rental properties in Tennessee, including:

- Single-family homes
- Apartments
- Condominiums
- Townhouses
- Duplexes and multi-unit properties

However, certain properties are exempt:
- Owner-occupied properties with 4 or fewer units
- Transitional housing
- Property held under government subsidized housing programs with different rules
- Some vacation rentals and short-term occupancies

Most landlords in Tennessee operate under the URLTA and must comply with its provisions.

### Key Provisions of Tennessee's URLTA

The URLTA covers critical areas including:

1. **Security deposits**: Handling, return timelines, and permissible deductions
2. **Landlord obligations**: Maintenance, repairs, and habitability
3. **Tenant obligations**: Rent payment, property care, and conduct
4. **Entry rights**: Notice requirements for landlord access
5. **Lease termination**: Notice periods and procedures
6. **Eviction procedures**: Legal process for removing tenants
7. **Remedies**: Available remedies for both parties when violations occur

Understanding these provisions is essential for drafting compliant Tennessee lease agreements.

## Essential Components of a Tennessee Lease Agreement

A comprehensive **Tennessee lease agreement** should include specific provisions to ensure clarity and legal compliance. While Tennessee doesn't mandate a particular lease format, certain elements are essential.

### Required and Recommended Lease Provisions

**Essential Lease Terms:**

1. **Parties**: Full legal names of landlord and all tenants
2. **Property description**: Complete address and description of rental unit
3. **Lease term**: Start and end dates, or month-to-month designation
4. **Rent amount**: Monthly rent and due date
5. **Payment method**: Where and how rent should be paid
6. **Security deposit**: Amount collected and terms
7. **Utilities**: Which party is responsible for each utility
8. **Maintenance responsibilities**: Division of repair obligations
9. **Entry rights**: Landlord's right to access with notice requirements
10. **Pet policy**: Whether pets are allowed and any associated fees
11. **Smoking policy**: Whether smoking is permitted
12. **Occupancy limits**: Maximum number of occupants

**Recommended Additional Provisions:**

- Late fee policy (must comply with Tennessee law)
- Renewal and termination procedures
- Subletting and assignment restrictions
- Property rules and regulations
- Noise and nuisance restrictions
- Parking arrangements
- Storage and common area access
- Required insurance (renter's insurance)
- Lead paint disclosure (for pre-1978 properties)
- Mold and mildew prevention responsibilities

### Written Lease vs. Oral Agreement

While Tennessee law allows oral lease agreements for tenancies under one year, **written leases are strongly recommended** for all rentals because they:

- Provide clear documentation of terms
- Help prevent disputes and misunderstandings
- Are easier to enforce in court
- Establish a professional landlord-tenant relationship
- Are required for leases longer than one year (per Statute of Frauds)

A well-drafted written lease is your best protection against future disputes.

## Tennessee Security Deposit Laws and Requirements

Security deposits are governed by **Tennessee Code §66-28-301**, which establishes rules for handling, returning, and deducting from deposits.

### No Statutory Deposit Limit in Tennessee

Unlike many states, Tennessee does **not impose a maximum limit** on security deposit amounts. Landlords may charge whatever deposit amount they deem necessary, though the amount should be:

- Reasonable for the property type and condition
- Clearly disclosed in the lease agreement
- Consistent with local market practices

However, some counties and municipalities may have local ordinances limiting deposits, so always check local regulations.

### Pet Deposits and Non-Refundable Fees

Tennessee law distinguishes between refundable security deposits and non-refundable fees:

- **Security deposits**: Refundable funds held to cover damages or unpaid rent
- **Non-refundable fees**: Clearly labeled fees that won't be returned (such as pet fees or cleaning fees)

If you charge non-refundable fees, they must be:
- Explicitly labeled as "non-refundable" in the lease
- Separately itemized from security deposits
- Reasonable in amount

### Security Deposit Return Timeline: 30 Days

Tennessee law requires landlords to return security deposits within **30 days** after the tenant vacates and surrenders possession of the property. The landlord must:

- Return the full deposit, or
- Provide an itemized statement of deductions with any remaining balance

The 30-day clock starts when:
- The tenant has completely moved out
- All keys have been returned
- The lease term has ended

### Itemization Requirements for Deductions

If withholding any portion of the security deposit, landlords must provide a detailed itemized list showing:

- Specific damages or issues (not vague descriptions)
- The cost to repair or clean each item
- Any unpaid rent or fees
- The calculation of total deductions

The itemization should include receipts or estimates for work performed. While Tennessee doesn't require receipts by statute, providing them helps defend against disputes.

### Permissible Deductions

Tennessee landlords may deduct from security deposits for:

1. **Unpaid rent**: Rent owed for the tenancy period
2. **Damage beyond normal wear and tear**: Physical damage caused by tenant or guests
3. **Unpaid utilities**: If tenant was responsible under the lease
4. **Cleaning costs**: Excessive cleaning beyond normal move-out cleaning
5. **Lease violations**: Costs from tenant breaches of lease terms
6. **Early termination costs**: If tenant broke lease without justification

Landlords **cannot** deduct for:
- Normal wear and tear (faded paint, worn carpet, minor scuffs)
- Pre-existing damage
- Routine maintenance
- Improvements or upgrades

### Consequences of Improper Security Deposit Handling

If a landlord fails to return the deposit or provide proper itemization within 30 days without good reason, Tennessee law allows tenants to:

- Sue in court for the deposit amount
- Potentially recover damages
- Recover court costs and attorney fees if they prevail

## Landlord Obligations Under Tennessee Law

Tennessee's URLTA imposes specific responsibilities on landlords to maintain rental properties and provide habitable living conditions.

### Implied Warranty of Habitability

Tennessee law includes an **implied warranty of habitability** (§66-28-304), requiring landlords to:

**Maintain Essential Services:**
- Adequate heating, cooling, and ventilation systems
- Hot and cold running water
- Functional plumbing and sewage systems
- Working electrical systems

**Ensure Structural Safety:**
- Weatherproof and watertight structure
- Safe floors, stairs, and railings
- Functional doors and windows with working locks
- Proper ventilation to prevent mold and moisture problems

**Provide Safe and Sanitary Conditions:**
- Working smoke detectors (required by law)
- Carbon monoxide detectors (if applicable)
- Freedom from pest infestations
- Proper trash removal facilities
- Compliance with applicable building and housing codes

### Landlord's Duty to Repair

Under **Tennessee Code §66-28-304**, landlords must make necessary repairs to maintain habitability. When a tenant notifies the landlord of a repair need:

1. Landlord must make repairs within a reasonable time
2. What's "reasonable" depends on the severity and nature of the problem
3. Emergency repairs (no heat, water, or sewage issues) require immediate attention
4. Non-emergency repairs typically should be completed within 14-30 days

### Entry Rights and Notice Requirements

Tennessee law requires landlords to provide **reasonable notice** before entering rental units under **§66-28-403**. While the statute doesn't specify an exact timeframe, **24 hours notice** is generally considered reasonable for:

- Routine inspections
- Necessary repairs and maintenance
- Showing the property to prospective tenants or buyers

Landlords may enter without notice only in:
- Emergencies (fire, flood, gas leak)
- When tenant has abandoned the property
- With tenant's consent to immediate entry

Repeated unreasonable entries can constitute harassment and violate the tenant's right to quiet enjoyment.

## Tenant Obligations and Responsibilities

While landlords must maintain properties, tenants also have important obligations under Tennessee's URLTA.

### Tenant's Duty to Pay Rent

The most fundamental tenant obligation is paying rent on time as specified in the lease. Tennessee law (§66-28-201) requires tenants to:

- Pay rent in full by the due date
- Pay at the location and manner specified in the lease
- Not withhold rent except for legally justifiable reasons

### Tenant's Maintenance Obligations

Under **§66-28-202**, tenants must:

- Keep the premises clean and sanitary
- Dispose of trash properly
- Use plumbing, electrical, and HVAC systems properly
- Not deliberately damage or allow guests to damage the property
- Notify landlord promptly of needed repairs
- Maintain smoke detectors (replace batteries)

### Prohibited Tenant Conduct

Tennessee tenants are prohibited from:

- Disturbing neighbors or creating nuisances
- Using the property for illegal purposes
- Allowing unauthorized persons to occupy the unit
- Making alterations without landlord permission
- Violating material lease terms

## Late Fees and Rent Increase Rules in Tennessee

Tennessee law provides some guidance on late fees and rent increases, though landlords have considerable flexibility.

### Late Fee Restrictions

While Tennessee doesn't set a specific maximum late fee amount, the fee must be:

- **Reasonable** and not excessive
- **Clearly stated** in the lease agreement
- Applied only after a grace period (typically 5 days is reasonable)
- Related to actual damages or administrative costs

Courts may strike down late fees that are punitive rather than compensatory. A common guideline is:
- **$10-$15 flat fee** for smaller rents, or
- **5-10% of monthly rent** with a reasonable cap

Always document your late fee policy clearly in the lease.

### Rent Increase Requirements

For **month-to-month tenancies**, Tennessee landlords must provide:
- **30 days notice** before increasing rent (customary, though not statutorily specified)
- Written notice of the increase

For **fixed-term leases**:
- Rent cannot be increased during the lease term unless the lease specifically allows it
- At lease renewal, landlords can propose new terms including higher rent

Tennessee has **no rent control laws**, so landlords can increase rent by any amount with proper notice (subject to anti-discrimination laws).

## Tennessee Eviction Procedures: Detainer Warrants

Tennessee's eviction process, officially called **unlawful detainer**, is governed by **§66-28-505 and related statutes**. The process is relatively landlord-friendly but requires following proper procedures.

### Grounds for Eviction

Tennessee landlords can evict tenants for:

1. **Non-payment of rent**: Most common reason
2. **Lease violations**: Material breaches of lease terms
3. **Illegal activity**: Criminal conduct on the premises
4. **Property damage**: Substantial damage beyond normal use
5. **Holdover tenancy**: Remaining after lease expires
6. **No cause**: For month-to-month tenancies with proper notice

### Notice Requirements

Before filing for eviction, landlords must provide proper notice:

**For Non-Payment of Rent:**
- **14-day Notice to Pay or Vacate**: Gives tenant 14 days to pay rent or move out
- Notice must state the amount owed and deadline

**For Lease Violations:**
- **30-day Notice to Cure or Vacate**: For violations that can be corrected
- **14-day Notice to Vacate**: For violations that can't be cured or serious violations

**For Terminating Month-to-Month Tenancy:**
- **30-day Notice**: To terminate without cause

### The Detainer Warrant Process

If the tenant doesn't comply with the notice:

1. **File Detainer Warrant**: Landlord files with General Sessions Court
2. **Service**: Sheriff or process server serves tenant (typically 6 days before hearing)
3. **Court Hearing**: Both parties present evidence
4. **Judgment**: Judge issues decision (typically same day)
5. **Writ of Possession**: If landlord wins, court issues writ
6. **Sheriff Removal**: Sheriff posts notice and removes tenant if they don't leave

The entire process typically takes 2-5 weeks in Tennessee, faster than most states.

### Self-Help Evictions Are Illegal

Tennessee strictly prohibits self-help evictions. Landlords cannot:

- Change locks without court order
- Remove tenant's belongings
- Shut off utilities
- Use force or intimidation
- Otherwise attempt to remove tenant without legal process

Violations can result in liability for actual and punitive damages, plus attorney fees.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Tennessee Lease Agreements

### Q1: Does Tennessee require a written lease agreement?

Leases for one year or longer must be in writing under the Statute of Frauds. While oral month-to-month agreements are legal, written leases are strongly recommended for all rentals to prevent disputes.

### Q2: How much can a landlord charge for a security deposit in Tennessee?

Tennessee has no statutory limit on security deposit amounts. However, the amount should be reasonable for the property and market. Some local jurisdictions may have limits, so check local ordinances.

### Q3: How long does a Tennessee landlord have to return a security deposit?

30 days after the tenant vacates and surrenders possession. The landlord must return the full deposit or provide an itemized statement of deductions with any remaining balance.

### Q4: Can Tennessee landlords charge late fees?

Yes, but late fees must be reasonable (not punitive), clearly stated in the lease, and applied only after a reasonable grace period. Typically 5-10% of rent or $10-$15 flat fee is considered reasonable.

### Q5: How much notice is required to terminate a month-to-month lease in Tennessee?

Either party can terminate with 30 days written notice. For fixed-term leases, the lease automatically expires at the end of the term unless renewed.
